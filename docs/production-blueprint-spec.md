# Production Blueprint Specification

## Purpose

Transform the creator’s validated Creative DNA, target-medium settings, and accepted adaptation recommendations into a practical short-film planning document.

The Production Blueprint is designed to help a creator move from analysis into pre-production without replacing screenplay writing, directing, or final creative judgment.

Prism should act as a structured creative partner, not an autonomous author.

---

## Inputs

The Production Blueprint is generated from:

* Validated Creative DNA
* Target-medium settings
* Accepted adaptation recommendations only
* Rejected recommendations, used only to confirm what must be excluded
* Pending recommendations, treated as unresolved unless the creator accepts them

---

## Decision Summary

Provide a concise overview of the human decisions that shaped the blueprint.

Include:

* total recommendations reviewed
* accepted recommendations used
* rejected recommendations excluded
* pending recommendations not applied
* unresolved creative decisions
* target format
* target duration
* intended platform

The summary must clearly show that the creator’s decisions control the final output.

---

## Adaptation Direction

Provide a concise statement describing the overall adaptation strategy.

Include:

* chosen adaptation approach
* central emotional promise of the source
* core premise to preserve
* primary themes to protect
* intended audience experience
* major adaptation priorities
* major creative boundaries

This section should explain what the short film is trying to preserve, emphasize, and transform.

---

## Source Traceability

Every major blueprint decision must be traceable to the creative material that informed it.

For each major scene, character priority, visual decision, pacing choice, or production recommendation, identify:

* the accepted recommendation that influenced it
* the Creative DNA element it protects or develops
* the relevant source evidence
* the adaptation principle being applied

Traceability must remain concise and readable.

The blueprint should make it clear why each major decision exists.

---

## Scene Roadmap

Provide a proposed sequence of 6 to 10 scenes for the short film.

Each scene must include:

### Scene Number

The scene’s order within the proposed film structure.

### Working Title

A short descriptive label for planning purposes.

### Purpose

The narrative function of the scene.

Examples include:

* establish the protagonist
* introduce the central conflict
* reveal a key relationship
* create a midpoint shift
* intensify emotional pressure
* resolve the central question

### Key Characters

The characters who appear or meaningfully influence the scene.

### Setting

The primary location and time context.

### Estimated Duration

An approximate runtime in minutes or seconds.

The total proposed runtime should remain reasonably aligned with the selected target duration.

### Major Emotional Beat

The main emotional movement or audience response created by the scene.

### Essential Story Information

The source material that must be communicated in this scene.

### Accepted Recommendations Applied

List the accepted recommendation IDs or titles that shaped the scene.

### Creative DNA Protected

List the relevant elements being preserved, such as:

* premise
* central conflict
* character arc
* primary theme
* emotional tone
* key symbol
* narrative turning point

### Visual or Sonic Opportunity

A brief note describing any meaningful visual, sound, music, or symbolic opportunity.

### Production Note

A practical consideration related to staging, location, cast, props, effects, or complexity.

---

## Character Priorities

Provide guidance for each major character.

Each character entry must include:

### Character Name

### Dramatic Function

The character’s purpose within the short-film adaptation.

### Essential Arc

The emotional or narrative change that must remain intact.

### Key Relationship

The relationship most important to the character’s role in the adaptation.

### Essential Traits

The qualities that must remain recognizable.

### Adaptation Focus

What should be emphasized, condensed, combined, or clarified for the short format.

### What Must Not Be Lost

The character element that is most important to preserve.

### Accepted Recommendations Applied

The accepted recommendations that influenced the character plan.

### Creative DNA Protected

The related archetype, arc, relationship, theme, conflict, or emotional function.

---

## Visual Language

Define the proposed visual identity of the adaptation.

Include:

* recurring visual motifs
* symbolic imagery
* color palette guidance
* lighting direction
* framing or composition tendencies
* camera movement philosophy
* environmental storytelling
* meaningful objects or props
* visual contrasts
* transitions or visual echoes

Each major visual recommendation should identify the Creative DNA element it supports.

---

## Sonic Language

Define the proposed sound identity of the adaptation.

Include:

* music direction
* ambient sound strategy
* recurring sound motifs
* silence and restraint
* dialogue density
* off-screen sound opportunities
* emotional sound transitions
* symbolic audio elements

The blueprint should not generate copyrighted lyrics or imitate a specific living artist.

---

## Pacing Plan

Describe how the short film should move from beginning to end.

Include:

### Opening Pace

How quickly the audience enters the story and what must be established first.

### Early Development

How the central relationship, conflict, or mystery gains momentum.

### Midpoint Shift

The event or realization that changes the direction or emotional pressure.

### Escalation

How tension, stakes, or emotional urgency increase.

### Climax Timing

Where the central confrontation or emotional peak should occur.

### Resolution

How much time should remain after the climax.

### Ending Rhythm

The emotional tempo and degree of closure in the final moments.

### Runtime Allocation

A rough distribution of minutes across the major structural sections.

---

## Production Considerations

Identify practical production needs and constraints.

Include:

* likely location requirements
* number of speaking roles
* background cast needs
* significant props
* wardrobe considerations
* lighting challenges
* sound-recording concerns
* visual effects requirements
* special effects requirements
* complex blocking
* night or exterior shooting
* weather dependence
* continuity concerns
* accessibility considerations
* likely high-cost elements
* opportunities to simplify without harming the story

Prism should distinguish between:

* essential production needs
* optional enhancements
* avoidable complexity

---

## Feasibility Notes

Assess whether the current adaptation plan is realistic for the selected format and duration.

Include:

* runtime feasibility
* number of scenes
* number of locations
* cast complexity
* production complexity
* likely resource pressure
* elements that may require compromise
* areas where scope should be reduced
* areas where the concept remains especially strong

This section should support realistic creative planning rather than simply praising the concept.

---

## Creative Risks and Safeguards

Identify the most important creative risks in the adaptation.

For each risk, include:

* risk description
* why it matters
* affected Creative DNA element
* likelihood
* potential impact
* mitigation strategy
* accepted recommendation related to the safeguard

Examples may include:

* emotional depth lost through compression
* symbolism becoming too literal
* a supporting character losing narrative purpose
* the climax arriving too quickly
* visual style overwhelming the story
* exposition replacing dramatic action

---

## Open Creative Questions

Provide a concise list of decisions that still require human judgment.

Each question should include:

* the unresolved choice
* why it matters
* the affected scene, character, theme, or production element
* available options
* likely tradeoffs
* whether the issue came from a pending recommendation
* whether the question should be resolved before screenplay drafting or before production

Prism must not silently decide these questions.

---

## Rejected Recommendation Record

Provide a brief record of recommendations the creator rejected.

For each rejected recommendation, include:

* recommendation title or ID
* proposed change
* reason it was excluded from the blueprint
* any downstream sections affected by its rejection

This section reinforces that rejected AI suggestions do not enter the final plan.

---

## Final Creative Handoff

End with a concise handoff summary for the creator.

Include:

* the adaptation’s central direction
* the most important preserved elements
* the strongest accepted creative decisions
* the largest remaining challenge
* the next recommended human action

Possible next actions may include:

* outline drafting
* screenplay drafting
* visual reference gathering
* budget review
* location scouting
* casting discussion
* director’s treatment
* production consultation

The handoff should make clear that the blueprint is the beginning of the next creative phase, not the finished work.

---

## Output Constraints

The Production Blueprint must:

* use accepted recommendations only
* exclude rejected recommendations
* treat pending recommendations as unresolved
* preserve creator intent
* remain consistent with the validated Creative DNA
* remain appropriate for the selected target medium
* remain reasonably aligned with the target runtime
* explain major decisions
* include source traceability
* separate facts, recommendations, and unresolved questions
* avoid pretending that uncertain assumptions are confirmed
* remain practical enough to support pre-production

---

## Prohibited Output

The Production Blueprint must not:

* generate a complete screenplay
* generate full dialogue scenes
* invent major plot events unsupported by the source
* override creator decisions
* apply rejected recommendations
* present unresolved choices as settled
* imitate a living creator’s exact style
* include copyrighted song lyrics
* fabricate production facts
* conceal uncertainty
* claim the adaptation is production-ready when major questions remain

---

## Success Criteria

A successful Production Blueprint should allow a creator to understand:

* what the short film is trying to become
* what must remain faithful to the source
* which recommendations were accepted
* which recommendations were rejected
* how the story could be structured visually and dramatically
* what practical production needs may arise
* what creative risks remain
* what decisions still belong to the human creator
* what the next concrete creative step should be

The final result should feel like a traceable, explainable, human-directed bridge between source material and pre-production.
